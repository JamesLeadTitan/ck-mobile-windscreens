Can You Drive With a Cracked Windscreen in the UK?

A cracked windscreen is one of those things that catches you off guard — a stone chip at 70mph and suddenly you're wondering whether your car is still legal to drive. The short answer is: it depends on the size and position of the crack. Here's exactly what UK law says and when you need to act fast.

What Does UK Law Say About Cracked Windscreens?

Under the Road Traffic Act, driving with a windscreen that obstructs your view is illegal. The Highway Code also states that all glass must be maintained in good condition. But the law doesn't give a specific measurement — instead it's enforced through MOT standards and police discretion.

If a crack impairs your visibility, you can be issued a fixed penalty notice and three penalty points. In serious cases, driving with a dangerously cracked windscreen could be classed as driving without due care and attention. Not worth the risk.

When Does a Cracked Windscreen Fail an MOT?

The MOT rules are more specific. Your windscreen will fail if:

  • There is damage larger than 10mm in the driver's central zone — a 290mm wide area directly in the driver's line of sight
  • Damage larger than 40mm anywhere else in the swept area (the area the wipers cover)
  • Any damage that causes a sharp edge or distorts the driver's vision

Even outside these zones, an advisory can be issued. And a crack that starts small can spread quickly — especially in cold weather when temperature changes put stress on the glass.

Chip vs Crack — Does It Make a Difference?

Yes. A chip is a small impact point, usually circular. Many chips — if caught early enough — can be repaired in under 30 minutes using UV-hardened resin, leaving the glass structurally sound and often virtually invisible.

A crack is a line in the glass that has spread from the impact point. Cracks are generally harder to repair and more likely to require a full windscreen replacement, particularly if they reach the edge of the glass or enter the driver's critical zone. The golden rule: get a chip repaired before it becomes a crack.

How Quickly Can a Crack Spread?

Faster than most people expect. Temperature changes, road vibration, and even slamming a car door can extend a crack within hours. A small chip left overnight in freezing temperatures can become a full-length crack by morning. If you've noticed damage, don't leave it — even a day can be the difference between a cheap repair and a full replacement.

What Should You Do If Your Windscreen Is Cracked?

First, assess the position and size. If it's outside the driver's critical zone and smaller than 40mm, you may be legal to drive — but you should still get it seen immediately. If it's in your direct line of sight or larger than 10mm in the central zone, avoid driving until it's assessed.
